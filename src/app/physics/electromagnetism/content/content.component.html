La historia del electromagnetismo es una narrativa fascinante que abarca varios siglos y ha involucrado a numerosos científicos e investigadores clave. Esta historia se puede dividir en varias secciones destacadas:

Descubrimiento de la Electricidad (Siglo XVII-XVIII)

El descubrimiento y comprensión de la electricidad se desarrolló a lo largo de los siglos XVII y XVIII, con contribuciones clave de varios científicos que realizaron experimentos y observaciones fundamentales. A continuación, se presentan algunos hitos importantes en la historia del descubrimiento de la electricidad durante ese período:

Guillaume Amontons (1663-1705):
 Realizó experimentos con electricidad estática, observando la atracción y repulsión de objetos electrificados.

Pieter van Musschenbroek (1692-1761): Inventó el condensador, también conocido como la botella de Leyden, un dispositivo para almacenar carga eléctrica.

Stephen Gray (1666-1736):
 Distinguió entre conductores y aislantes. Descubrió que algunos materiales podían transportar la electricidad.

Ewald Georg von Kleist (1700-1748): También contribuyó al desarrollo del condensador de Leyden al mismo tiempo que Musschenbroek.

Charles François de Cisternay du Fay (1698-1739):
 Propuso la existencia de dos tipos de electricidad, a la que llamó "vítreo" (positiva) y "resinoso" (negativa).

Joseph Priestley (1733-1804):
 Descubrió que ciertos conductores podrían descargar la electricidad de un condensador.

Benjamin Franklin (1706-1790):
 * Contribuyó significativamente a la comprensión de la electricidad.
* Realizó experimentos con cometas para demostrar la naturaleza eléctrica de los rayos.
* Propuso el modelo de un fluido eléctrico y desarrolló la teoría de la electricidad de un solo fluido.

Henry Cavendish (1731-1810):
 Desarrolló la teoría de que la electricidad es un fluido compuesto por partículas eléctricas individuales.








Desarrollo de la Teoría Eléctrica (Siglo XVIII-XIX)

Galileo Galilei (1564-1642): Galileo performed pioneering experiments on the movement and fall of objects. He discovered that all objects, regardless of their mass, fall at the same rate in a uniform gravitational field. He also formulated the laws of motion and laid the foundation for the law of inertia.






Investigaciones en Magnetismo (Siglo XVIII-XIX)

Isaac Newton (1643-1727): Newton consolidated Galileo's ideas and formulated the laws of motion and the law of universal gravitation. His contributions revolutionized physics and astronomy, providing a mathematical framework for understanding and predicting the motion of objects.







Unificación de Electricidad y Magnetismo (Siglo XIX)

Three-body problem: The gravitational interaction between three celestial bodies is a classic problem in celestial mechanics that still has no general analytical solution. The complexity of this problem has led to the development of numerical methods and the study of chaotic systems.

Teoría Cuántica y Electrodinámica Cuántica (Siglo XX)

Rigid body problem: The study of rotating rigid bodies has led to the development of rigid mechanics and has found applications in engineering and vehicle dynamics.

Innovaciones Contemporáneas (Siglo XXI)

Einstein's theory of relativity: In the early 20th century, Albert Einstein proposed the theory of relativity, which transformed the understanding of gravity. The theory of general relativity describes gravity as a curvature of space-time caused by the presence of mass and energy.







Quantum mechanics

Quantum mechanics: At the subatomic level, quantum mechanics replaced classical mechanics. Developed in the first half of the 20th century, quantum mechanics describes the behavior of subatomic particles and has led to technologies such as quantum electronics.

Double-slit experiment

In modern physics, the double-slit experiment demonstrates that light and matter can satisfy the seemingly incongruous classical definitions for both waves and particles, which is considered evidence for the fundamentally probabilistic nature of quantum mechanics. This type of experiment was first performed by Thomas Young in 1801, as a demonstration of the wave behavior of visible light. At that time it was thought that light consisted of either waves or particles. With the beginning of modern physics, about a hundred years later, it was realized that light could in fact show both wave and particle characteristics. In 1927, Davisson and Germer and, independently George Paget Thomson and his research student Alexander Reid demonstrated that electrons show the same behavior, which was later extended to atoms and molecules. Thomas Young's experiment with light was part of classical physics long before the development of quantum mechanics and the concept of wave–particle duality. He believed it demonstrated that Christiaan Huygens' wave theory of light was correct, and his experiment is sometimes referred to as Young's experiment or Young's slits.
